1.	EMS

EMS stands for Equipment Maintenance Strategy. It is a structured and standardized approach used to define how each type of equipment should be maintained throughout its lifecycle, based on its function, failure risks, criticality, and operational context. The objective of EMS is to ensure that each equipment type has a tailored, risk-based maintenance plan that:

	Prevents failures
	Ensures safety and compliance
	Optimized costs
	Supports reliability and asset integrity
	
An EMS typically defines:

o	Item Class: Functional category of equipment (e.g., PUCE – Centrifugal Pump). 

o	Scope: What the equipment is designed to do.

o	Boundary: interface between an item and its surroundings.

o	FMEA Basis: What can go wrong, and how it should be mitigated.

o	Maintenance Strategy Overview: Justification and logic behind the selected PM approach.

o	Work Task Templates (WTTs): Standardized tasks with frequency based on the Object Criticality, Reporting Questions, Work Instructions, and resources. 

Work Task template is one of the inputs for FMEA construction.

o	Reporting Questions: Pass/fail or performance checks that support assurance and compliance of “what to do” in a routine. 
It can be two types: Assurance Activity that comes from Performance Standard or Reporting Question – both will be defined in the next Chapters


2.	ITEM CLASS
The item class groups equipment by the functional similarity and is equivalent to the Equipment Class defined in ISO 14224.

Exemple: DREM for Eletrical Motor

3.	EQUIPMENT FUNCTION
An equipment function is the intended role or purpose that a piece of equipment performs within a system or process. It describes what the equipment is designed to perform.

Example for DREM – Electrical Motor: Convert electrical energy into mechanical energy.


4.	BOUNDARY

The purpose of the boundary definition is to ensure a common understanding of the “subunit/component” and “maintainable item/part” included within the boundary of a particular equipment unit. 

The FMEA should consider what is inside the boundaries of EMS to build the Maintainable Items.
EMS is one of the inputs for FMEA construction.

5.	FUNCTIONAL FAILURE
A functional failure is the inability of an item to fulfill a function to a standard of performance which is acceptable to the user.
So, a functional failure doesn’t necessarily mean the equipment is physically broken — it means it can no longer perform its intended function as required in its current operating context.
It’s about loss of function, not necessarily loss of motion.

6.	MAINTAINABLE ITEM

Item that constitutes a part or an assembly of parts that is normally the lowest level in the equipment hierarchy during maintenance. 
The group of parts of the equipment unit that are commonly maintained (repaired/ restored) as a whole.
E.g: Cooler, coupling, gearbox, lubrication oil pump, instrument loop, Seal, tube, shell, impeller, gasket, filter plate

The Standard for “Maintainable Item” will follow the structure below as Output:
After every Maintainable Item must be followed by the word “Failure”.

Maintainable Item + “Failure”
Example: “NDE Bearing Failure”

The Maintainable Item is part of the event that caused the Functional Failure, it’s the item present in the Failure – Example: “Bearing Failure”
7.	FAILURE EFFECT
Failure effects describe what happens when each failure mode occurs.
They should include all the information needed to support the evaluation of the consequences of that failure.

CRITICAL GUIDANCE: ELU vs ELP SYMPTOM SELECTION (ISO 14224)

The distinction between ELU (External leakage - utility medium) and ELP (External leakage - process medium) is critical and must be based on the FUNCTION of the fluid, not just the fluid type.

**Decision Criteria:**

A) ELP - External Leakage - Process Medium
   - Use when: The fluid IS THE PRODUCT or is part of the MAIN OPERATIONAL PROCESS
   - Fluid examples: Crude oil being pumped, natural gas being compressed, injection water for wells, process water, produced fluids
   - Equipment examples: Process pumps, compressors, process valves, process piping, separators, pressure vessels, heat exchangers (process side)
   - Function: Equipment is DESIGNED TO CONTAIN, TRANSPORT, OR PROCESS this fluid as its primary purpose
   - ISO 14224 Reference: Tables B.6 to B.13 show ELP for equipment with containment/process function

B) ELU - External Leakage - Utility Medium
   - Use when: The fluid SUPPORTS the equipment operation but IS NOT the main process fluid
   - Fluid examples: Lubricating oil, cooling water, service air, hydraulic oil, seal support fluids
   - Equipment examples: Lubrication systems, cooling circuits, hydraulic actuators, pneumatic systems (service air)
   - Function: Equipment uses these fluids to ENABLE operation (lubrication, cooling, control) but NOT as the process medium
   - ISO 14224 Reference: Tables B.6 to B.13 show ELU for auxiliary/support systems

C) When ELU/ELP DO NOT APPLY
   - Equipment without any fluid circuits: Junction boxes, terminal boxes, cable glands, dry electrical enclosures
   - Purely electrical/electronic equipment with no lubricating, cooling, or process fluids
   - Logic/control systems without hydraulic or pneumatic actuation

**Examples:**

✅ CORRECT Applications:
1. Centrifugal Pump (crude oil service):
   - Symptom: ELP - External leakage - process medium (crude oil is the PROCESS fluid being pumped)
   - Symptom: ELU - External leakage - utility medium (lube oil leak from bearing housing - SUPPORTS pump operation)

2. Electric Motor:
   - Symptom: ELU - External leakage - utility medium (cooling water from heat exchanger, lube oil from bearings)
   - NOT ELP: Motor does not contain process fluids

3. Gas Compressor:
   - Symptom: ELP - External leakage - process medium (natural gas leak from seal - gas is PROCESS medium)
   - Symptom: ELU - External leakage - utility medium (lube oil leak, seal oil leak - SUPPORT fluids)

4. Control Valve (on process line):
   - Symptom: ELP - External leakage - process medium (process fluid leak from packing/body)
   - Symptom: ELU - External leakage - utility medium (instrument air leak from actuator - SUPPORT fluid)

❌ INCORRECT Applications:
1. Junction Box:
   - NOT ELU: No fluids present - junction box only isolates electrical connections
   - NOT ELP: No process fluids
   - Valid symptoms: STD (Structural deficiency), PTF (Power transmission failure), etc.

2. Cable Termination Box:
   - NOT ELU: No utility fluids
   - NOT ELP: No process fluids
   - Valid symptoms: Electrical failures only

3. Instrument Transmitter (pressure/temperature):
   - ONLY ELU if it has pneumatic supply (service air leak)
   - NOT ELP unless mounted on process piping and body integrity fails

**Key Decision Question:**
"What is the PRIMARY FUNCTION of this fluid in relation to the equipment?"
- If answer is "To be contained/transported/processed by the equipment" → ELP
- If answer is "To support the equipment operation (cool/lubricate/actuate)" → ELU
- If answer is "There is no fluid" → Neither ELU nor ELP applies

9.	CAUSE

The Causes are defined following the ISO 14224 Failure Mechanisms Table B.2 and it’s connected to the Maintainable Item.
The failure mechanism is the physical, chemical or other process or combination of processes that leads to the failure. It is an attribute of the failure event that can be deduced technically, e.g. the apparent, observed cause of the failure.
It’s the observable cause why the Maintainable Item (such as a replaceable part within a system) fails with that Symptom.
Once it’s known why a Failure Mechanism occurs in that specific Maintainable Item, it’s possible to find an effective way to prevent, monitor or predict it.

The “Other” and “Unknown” causes should be avoided, so it should not be part of the Cause list in IFS.
If there is not sufficient information to apply codes at this sublevel, then codes on the main level as listed above may be used.

10.	TREATMENT ACTION
Treatment Action is a set of recommended actions which are designed to ensure that each asset continues to fulfill its functions.
During the operation, action can be taken to detect the Symptom, or imminent failure, to prevent or reduce its effects.
The Maintenance Strategy can also be used as a means of control and should be developed in a structured manner from the Failure Tree. The treatments may result in one or more of the following:

o	Prevent functional failures before they occur (if possible).
o	Predict potential failures early enough to act.
o	Find hidden failures in protective systems.
o	Redesign the asset or process if proactive tasks are not effective.
Allow run-to-failure where appropriate.


It’s important to have a brief of any recommendation taken, this can be identified in the WTT Application and/or FASN Maintenance Strategy Overview.
Some questions should be considered during the Treatment Action selection.
o	Is there a technically valid way to predict or prevent failure? 
Examples: Restoring the item to its original condition at a specified interval (overhaul) or Inspecting or monitoring the item to detect a potential failure before functional failure occurs or Replacing an item or part at a fixed interval before failure.
o	Is it evident or hidden? If It’s Hidden Failure, A periodic test or inspection to detect hidden failures
o	Is the task worth doing? In terms of Cost/Benefit → If not justified: Redesign or Run-to Failure

ASSURANCE ACTIVITY

1.	Performance Criteria are the objective, auditable, and measurable parameters that a barrier (Safety Critical Element - SECE) must achieve to ensure its intended function.

2.	Assurance Activities are the systematic set of tasks performed to confirm that the Performance Criteria of an SECE are being achieved.
Assurance Activities shall be planned, executed, and documented in accordance with the Performance Standards, ensuring the required Performance Criteria defined for the barrier is attended during the Operation Phase.

3.	Pass/Fail Criteria are the predefined and measurable conditions (Questions format) that establish whether an SECE meets or fails to meet its Performance Criteria. They serve as explicit acceptable range, ensuring that assurance activities yield a clear and auditable outcome (Pass or Fail).
Each Performance Criteria shall have corresponding Pass/Fail Criteria, ensuring that compliance can be objectively demonstrated.

WHAT IS NOT AN ASSURANCE ACTIVITY
Activity that should not be considered as Pass Fail:

Administrative or Paperwork Activities
•	Example: Registering maintenance completion in IFS / CMMS.
•	Reason: This is a procedural compliance step, not a direct demonstration of the SCE’s ability to meet its Performance Criteria.
Document Reviews or Approvals
•	Example: Approving work permits, signing inspection checklists, validating vendor certificates.
•	Reason: They provide governance and traceability but are not pass/fail tests of barrier integrity.
Training and Competence Records
•	Example: Confirming that a technician attended a safety training course.
•	Reason: Important for workforce assurance, but it does not validate the SCE’s operational performance.
General Compliance Checks
•	Example: Confirming that procedures were followed step-by-step.
•	Reason: Procedure adherence is vital, but it should not be confused with technical pass/fail of the barrier.
Housekeeping or General Condition Reporting
•	Example: “Area clean and free from obstacles,” “labels in place,” or “paint condition OK.”
•	Reason: These contribute to good practice and maintainability but are not performance validation criteria for the SCE itself.
Design Criteria
•	Example of Performance Criteria covered by design: “Piping design & routing for lines containing Choke valve to be designed in line with P&IDs & Supplier GA drawing provided for Choke valve.” 
•	Reason: These are Criteria covered in the Design Phase and should not be checked during the Operation Phase with an Assurance Activity and consequently Pass/Fail.


•	Technical Reporting (TR)
Technical Reporting is structured based on the work instructions outlined in each WTT.
The primary objective of AA and TR is to clearly communicate to the executor “What needs to be performed” and “Which part which it should be executed”. This ensures clarity in task execution, minimizing uncertainties. 
If the executor has any questions regarding how to execute the task, they can refer to the attached document linked to the WTT.

The FMEA must be able to tell whether the Reporting Questions are adequate, whether there is a lack of coverage of any cause.
Or even whether a reporting question is being asked unnecessarily, i.e., it does not cover any failure Mechanism, or there is already another reporting question in the same item class that already covers the same cause.

B)	HOW TO BUILD A TREATMENT ACTION OR TECHNICAL REPORTING
The Reporting Question must be a positive question, which could have a tolerance/limit value or binary answer. 

**If the work instruction is not giving the correct information or there is room for improvement, a review of the Work Instruction will be considered during the FMEA Exercise.
 
** If the tasks inside the Work Instruction is classified as “If applicable”, so do not include in the Technical Reporting.

Assurance Activity will follow the Performance Standard inputs.

In the context of an Assurance Activity (originating from the PS and associated with a safety critical element- SECE), the categorization is designated as Pass/Fail criteria. 
Conversely, when not associated with an Assurance Activity, the categorization is classified as Technical Reporting.

The construction of the Question will adhere to the following structure:
Structure details to be added as required:

“Terminal boxes sealed and free of damaged? [Y/N]”
“The cables insulation, cable glands, cable grounding, drive coupling,           sprocket, pulleys and belts free of damaged? [Y/N]”

Trip in the compressor



V.	Symptom Code
Symptom Code is the same failure modes presented in the Symptom Catalog file, based on ISO 14224, table B.15.
If the Item Class is not listed in the Symptom Catalog, please suggest Symptoms using as reference this is a FMEA for an Oil and Gas industry, where the equipment is part of an FPSO.

Rules: 
a)	Should one symptom per line be considered.
b)	Keep all symptoms possible per maintainable item following the Symptom Catalog file.
c)	More than one symptom can be listed per Maintainable Item.
d)	All the Maintainable Item should have at least one Symptom.

e)	Symptom–Item Consistency Rule
When generating the FMEA, the AI must analyze each Maintainable Item’s technical nature (mechanical, electrical, instrumentation, sealing, structural, control/software, etc.) using the EMS boundaries and Maintainable Item Catalog.
Then, for each Maintainable Item, only select Symptoms that are physically or functionally observable for that type according to ISO 14224.
Exclude or replace Symptoms that are not technically coherent (e.g., "Failure to start" on bearings → use "Failure to rotate"; "Leakage" on control panels → invalid).
This validation must be done before final FMEA export to guarantee that every Symptom is realistic for the Maintainable Item’s actual function and engineering behavior.

Application Rules of Symptoms for AI Agent
a)	Technical Classification of Maintainable Item
Before assigning any Symptom, the AI shall determine the Maintainable Item’s technical type based on the EMS boundary, Maintainable Item Catalog, and functional description.
Each Maintainable Item shall be classified into one of the following example types:

o	Mechanical (Rotating / Static)
o	Sealing System
o	Electrical / Power Supply
o	Control / Software / Logic
o	Instrumentation / Measurement
o	Safety Equipment (Lifeboats, lifebuoys…)

b)	Symptom Eligibility
Only Symptoms that are physically or functionally observable for the given type of Maintainable Item shall be selected from the Symptom Catalog (ISO 14224).
The AI agent must ensure that each chosen Symptom can reasonably be detected during operation, inspection, or maintenance of that item.

c)	Invalid Symptom Exclusion
Symptoms that are not technically applicable to the Maintainable Item’s physical nature must be automatically excluded.
Examples:
o	“Failure to start on demand (FTS)” → applies only to systems or components with starting or power-supply function (e.g., motors, PLCs).
	For rotating mechanical parts such as bearings, replace with “Failure to rotate (FRO)”.
o	“External Leakage (ELP)” → applies only to pressure-retaining or sealing components (e.g., seals, casings).
o	“Overheating (OHE)” → applicable to mechanical or electrical items with heat generation; not valid for logic or instrumentation items.
o	“Control failure (CSF)” → valid for electronic or programmable components; not valid for mechanical parts.

d)	Symptom Replacement Logic
When an invalid Symptom is identified, it must be replaced by the closest technically valid alternative according to the failure behavior of that item.

e)	Validation Rule
For each selected Symptom, the AI shall internally validate the following statement:
“Can this Symptom be observed or detected in this Maintainable Item during its operation or maintenance?”
If the answer is No, the Symptom shall not be included in the output.
f)	Documentation of Replacements
When a Symptom is replaced for technical coherence, a brief justification should be noted in the FMEA development log (e.g., “FTS replaced by FRO for Bearing — mechanical seizure; no electrical function involved”).
This traceability ensures engineering rationale and auditability.

Expected Outcome for Symptoms
•	All Symptoms are technically valid and observable for their Maintainable Items.
•	No mismatches such as electrical symptoms on mechanical components, or leakage symptoms on control equipment.
•	The FMEA output represents the actual physical behavior of each Maintainable Item within the EMS boundary.



VI.	Failure Mechanism
Rules: 

a)	Failure Mechanism describe the specific processes, events, or conditions that lead to the symptom happen in that specific Maintainable Item.

b)	The Failure Mechanism is based in the Table B.2 – Failure Mechanism, there is no room for interpretation in this case, the output of the Failure Mechanism must be the Code number (sub) + Notation. Example of a Failure Mechanism in the output file: 
1.5 Looseness

c)	The ‘General’ Code number (sub) should be avoided, but it can be used in cases where no other option fits better. For these cases use the combination: Failure mechanism + Code number (sub) + Notation. Example: 
Mechanical failure 1 General
Electrical failure 1 General

d)	More than one failure mechanism can be listed per Maintainable Item.

The Output file must be a XLXS file with the following columns: Item Class Id, Function, Maintainable Item, Maintainable Item Function, Symptom, Failure Mechanism, Failure Effect, Treatment Actions, Reporting Question ID.
The rules to build these columns are all defined inside the chapter “11. OUTPUT FMEA STRUCTURE” from item I. until VII.
